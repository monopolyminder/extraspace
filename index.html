Step by Step

The Bait & Switch Playbook

Extra Space Storage doesn't make money by offering you a good deal on storage. They make money by getting you in the door cheap, then jacking up the price once you're stuck. This is how the playbook works.

01

Advertise a Low "No Contract" Rate

Units are advertised at low introductory rates. The phrase "no long-term contract" sounds like it's for your benefit, but it actually means they can raise your rate at any time, and they will. A real lease would lock in your rate. "No contract" just means you have no protection.

02

Lock You In with Friction

You pay non-refundable admin fees, buy a lock, rent a truck, and spend a day hauling your stuff in. Now moving it all back out would cost you almost as much as the first few months of rent. They're counting on that.

03

Raise the Rate. Fast.

Within months, your rate starts climbing. NYC's DCWP documented a case where a customer's rent jumped 165% in three months, from $290/mo to $479/mo. Another went from $120 to $320 in a single month. These increases have nothing to do with inflation. They're calculated by an algorithm designed to squeeze you for as much as possible.

04

Trap You in a Lose-Lose

Now you're stuck. You can keep paying a rate that might be more than your stuff is even worth. You can spend the time and money to move everything out, possibly to another facility they also own. Or you can just abandon your belongings. A lot of people end up doing all three.

Behind the Curtain

The Pricing Algorithm

Extra Space Storage doesn't set prices the way a normal business does. They use what they call "Adaptive Dynamic Pricing and Discounting Management" (DPDM). It's a system built to figure out the highest possible price they can charge you before you leave.

What They Claim

  • × "Rate increases reflect market conditions"
  • × "We're almost always at full capacity"
  • × "Prices are competitive with the local market"

What the Evidence Shows

  • ✓ NYC's lawsuit states increases "have no correlation to any market conditions or costs"
  • ✓ They aggressively advertise empty units while claiming to be at capacity
  • ✓ They buy up competitors to become the local market

How the Algorithm Works Against You

In a 2009 academic paper published in the International Journal of Revenue Management, Extra Space Storage's own team described their pricing system. The paper explains how the algorithm "dynamically adjust[s]" pricing policy based on "store performance," not on costs or market rates. The stated goal is to "improve store revenue growth" while "maintain[ing] high occupancy."

Translation: the algorithm figures out the highest price each location can charge before enough people leave to hurt occupancy. It doesn't respond to market conditions. It calculates how much pain customers will put up with before they walk.

Market Control

The algorithm only works if you can't easily go somewhere else. That's why Extra Space has spent billions buying up competitors. In many areas, they don't just compete in the market. They are the market.

Why This Matters to You

When Extra Space raises your rate 50%, 100%, or even 165%, your first thought is to shop around. But in a lot of markets, the "competing" facility down the road is also owned by Extra Space, or managed by them under a different name. You think you have options. You might not. Even facilities with completely different branding can be Extra Space properties running the same pricing algorithm.

Hidden in Plain Sight

What Most People Don't Know

The "No Contract" Is the Contract

Month-to-month sounds flexible. What it really means is they can raise your rate every single month with minimal notice. A traditional lease would actually protect you by locking in a rate. "No contract" is marketed as a benefit. It's their most powerful weapon.

They Published How the Algorithm Works

In 2009, Extra Space's team published a paper in the International Journal of Revenue Management describing their "Adaptive Dynamic Pricing and Discounting Management" system. The paper openly states the goal: maximize revenue growth per store. Not better service. Not lower costs for you. Just more money out of every location.

Third-Party Management Hides Their Reach

On top of the 4,000+ properties they own, Extra Space manages 1,856 additional stores for other owners. These facilities operate under different names but run the same pricing algorithms and practices. You think you're picking an independent option. You're not.

Forced Arbitration Silences Victims

When customers do try to sue, Extra Space's rental agreements often include mandatory arbitration clauses. This forces disputes into private proceedings instead of public courts, preventing class actions and keeping the pattern of abuse out of the public record. The Ionescu case in California was sent to arbitration over the plaintiff's objection.

They Threaten to Auction Your Belongings

According to the NYC lawsuit, when customers push back on rate increases or fall behind on inflated payments, Extra Space has threatened to auction off their stored property. Some customers reported locks being changed and belongings cleared without proper legal notice.

The "Full Capacity" Claim Doesn't Add Up

Extra Space frequently claims high occupancy to justify rate hikes. But they simultaneously spend heavily on advertising empty units with promotional rates. You can't be nearly full and also be running ads for discounted units. One of those stories is a lie, and the pricing algorithm tells you which one.

Take Action

How to Protect Yourself

Before You Rent

  • Search for independently owned storage facilities in your area
  • Ask explicitly: "Is this facility owned or managed by Extra Space Storage?"
  • Request a written commitment on rate stability. If they won't give one, walk away
  • Read the full rental agreement, especially arbitration and rate-increase clauses
  • Calculate the true cost: assume your rate will at least double within a year

If You're Already a Customer

  • Document every rate increase with dates and screenshots
  • File a complaint with your state Attorney General's office
  • File a complaint with the Better Business Bureau
  • File a complaint with the Federal Trade Commission
  • Contact your local consumer protection agency
  • Consider moving your belongings before the next increase
