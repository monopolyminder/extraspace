Step by Step

The Bait & Switch Playbook

Extra Space Storage's model isn't about providing affordable storage. It's a carefully engineered cycle designed to trap customers into escalating costs. Here's how the playbook works, step by step.

01

Advertise a Low "No Contract" Rate

Units are marketed at aggressively low introductory rates. The phrase "no long-term contract" sounds consumer-friendly, but it actually means they can raise your rate at any time — and they will. This language is deliberately designed to sound like freedom while removing your protection.

02

Lock You In with Friction

You pay non-refundable administration fees, buy a lock, rent a truck, and spend a day moving your belongings in. The physical and financial cost of moving everything back out creates enormous switching costs — and they know it.

03

Raise the Rate — Fast and Hard

Within months, your rate starts climbing. NYC's DCWP documented a case where a customer's rent jumped 165% in three months — from $290/mo to $479/mo. Another saw a single-month spike from $120 to $320. These aren't inflation adjustments. They're algorithmic extractions.

04

Trap You in a Lose-Lose

Now you face an impossible choice: pay a rate that may exceed the value of your belongings, spend the time and money to move out (to another facility they may also own), or abandon your property entirely. Many customers end up doing all three.

Behind the Curtain

The Pricing Algorithm

Extra Space Storage doesn't set prices the way a normal business does. They use what they call "Adaptive Dynamic Pricing and Discounting Management" (DPDM) — a system explicitly designed to maximize revenue extraction from each individual customer.

What They Claim

  • × "Rate increases reflect market conditions"
  • × "We're almost always at full capacity"
  • × "Prices are competitive with the local market"

What the Evidence Shows

  • ✓ NYC's lawsuit states increases "have no correlation to any market conditions or costs"
  • ✓ They aggressively advertise empty units while claiming to be at capacity
  • ✓ They buy up competitors to become the local market

How the Algorithm Works Against You

In a 2009 academic paper published in the International Journal of Revenue Management, Extra Space Storage's own team described their pricing system. The paper explains how the algorithm "dynamically adjust[s]" pricing policy based on "store performance" — not on costs or market rates. The stated goal is to "improve store revenue growth" while "maintain[ing] high occupancy."

In plain language: the algorithm calculates the maximum price each location can charge before enough people leave to hurt occupancy. It's not responding to market conditions — it's engineering the maximum amount of pain a customer base will tolerate.

Why This Matters to You

When Extra Space raises your rate 50%, 100%, or even 165%, your natural instinct is to shop around. But in many markets, the "competing" facility down the road is also owned by Extra Space — or managed by them through their third-party management platform. The illusion of choice is part of the business model. Even facilities operating under different brand names may be Extra Space properties.

Hidden in Plain Sight

What Most People Don't Know

The "No Contract" Is the Contract

Month-to-month sounds flexible. In reality, it means they can raise your rate every single month with minimal notice. A traditional lease would actually protect you by locking in a rate. "No contract" is marketed as a benefit — it's their most powerful weapon.

They Published How the Algorithm Works

In 2009, Extra Space's team published a paper in the International Journal of Revenue Management describing their "Adaptive Dynamic Pricing and Discounting Management" system. The paper openly states the goal: maximize revenue growth per store. Not serve customers. Not respond to costs. Maximize extraction.

Third-Party Management Hides Their Reach

Beyond the 4,000+ properties they own, Extra Space manages 1,856 additional stores for other owners. These facilities may operate under different names but use the same pricing algorithms and practices. You might think you're choosing an independent alternative — you're not.

Forced Arbitration Silences Victims

When customers do try to sue, Extra Space's rental agreements often include mandatory arbitration clauses. This forces disputes into private proceedings instead of public courts, preventing class actions and keeping the pattern of abuse out of the public record. The Ionescu case in California was sent to arbitration over the plaintiff's objection.

They Threaten to Auction Your Belongings

According to the NYC lawsuit, when customers push back on rate increases or fall behind on inflated payments, Extra Space has threatened to auction off their stored property. Some customers reported locks being changed and belongings cleared without proper legal notice.

The "Full Capacity" Claim Doesn't Add Up

Extra Space frequently claims high occupancy to justify rate hikes. But they simultaneously spend heavily on advertising empty units with promotional rates. You can't be nearly full and also be aggressively soliciting new renters at discounted prices. One of those stories is a lie — and the pricing algorithm reveals which one.

Take Action

How to Protect Yourself

Before You Rent

  • Search for independently owned storage facilities in your area
  • Ask explicitly: "Is this facility owned or managed by Extra Space Storage?"
  • Request a written commitment on rate stability — if they won't give one, walk away
  • Read the full rental agreement, especially arbitration and rate-increase clauses
  • Calculate the true cost: assume your rate will at least double within a year

If You're Already a Customer

  • Document every rate increase with dates and screenshots
  • File a complaint with your state Attorney General's office
  • File a complaint with the Better Business Bureau
  • File a complaint with the Federal Trade Commission
  • Contact your local consumer protection agency
  • Consider moving your belongings before the next increase

Spread the Word

  • Leave honest, detailed reviews on Google, Yelp, and the BBB
  • Contact your local news station — consumer reporters cover these stories
  • Share on social media to help others avoid the trap
  • Support legislation requiring price transparency in self-storage
